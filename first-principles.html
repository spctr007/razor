01 / Foundation

What is First
Principles Thinking?

It's the practice of breaking a complex problem down to its most fundamental, irreducible truths — then building your solution back up from those truths, rather than from analogy, convention, or cargo-culting what everyone else does.

Reasoning by first principles is the practice of actively questioning every assumption you think you know about a given problem, and then creating new knowledge and solutions from scratch.

— Inspired by Aristotle, popularized by Elon Musk and applied engineers everywhere


In software, this means asking: why does this architecture exist? What problem does it fundamentally solve? If I had to build this from scratch knowing only the constraints, what would I build?


With AI tools, this matters more than ever — because AI can generate plausible-looking code that is completely wrong for your context if you don't understand the ground truth yourself.

02 / Core Framework

The 5 Principles
Applied to Software

Click each principle to expand it with software engineering examples and how AI fits in.

PRINCIPLE 01
Question Every Assumption
Don't accept "we've always done it this way" as a technical justification.

Every architectural decision carries hidden assumptions. REST APIs, SQL databases, microservices — these are solutions to specific historical problems. Before adopting them, ask: what problem am I actually solving? Are these constraints still true for me?

Software Example

Assumption: "We need a microservices architecture." Question: Do we actually have team scaling or deployment independence problems, or are we just following trends? For a 3-person team, a modular monolith might be fundamentally better.

AI Application

When AI suggests a pattern, prompt it: "What assumptions does this solution make about my scale, team, and constraints? What would you build differently if those assumptions were false?"

PRINCIPLE 02
Decompose to Fundamentals
Break the problem until you hit atoms — things that are undeniably true.

Keep asking "why?" and "what does this actually need to do?" until you reach irreducible requirements. These are your constraints. Everything else is a design choice — and design choices can be changed.

Software Example

"Build a user authentication system." Fundamentals: (1) Verify identity. (2) Persist session. (3) Protect resources. From these atoms, you decide: OAuth vs passwords, JWT vs sessions, etc. — based on YOUR context.

AI Application

Ask AI: "Help me decompose this feature into its fundamental requirements — what does it absolutely need to do, ignoring implementation?" This forces clarity before coding starts.

PRINCIPLE 03
Reconstruct from Truth
Build your solution up from fundamentals, not down from convention.

Once you know your irreducible truths, design flows naturally and intentionally. You're not copying a pattern — you're choosing components that solve each fundamental requirement. This means you understand every piece of your system.

Software Example

Fundamental: "I need to process 10 events/sec with guaranteed delivery." From this, you reconstruct: do I need a queue? What durability guarantee? Now Redis streams, RabbitMQ, or even a DB-backed queue all become conscious choices, not cargo cult.

AI Application

After decomposing, use AI to explore reconstruction: "Given these 4 fundamental constraints, generate 3 different architectures and explain which assumptions each makes."

PRINCIPLE 04
Separate Problem from Solution
Most "technical problems" are actually solution-framing problems.

When someone says "we need to optimize this SQL query," they've already anchored to a solution. The actual problem might be "users wait too long." That problem might be solved without touching SQL at all — caching, pagination, async loading.

Software Example

"Our deployment pipeline is too slow" → Is the problem build time? Feedback loop length? Fear of deploying? Each has a different first-principles solution. Don't optimize the wrong thing faster.

AI Application

Tell AI the problem, not the solution. "Users are complaining about slowness" vs "optimize my database query." The first framing lets AI think from first principles with you.

PRINCIPLE 05
Validate Against Reality
First principles reasoning must be tested, not just trusted.

Even sound logic can miss real-world complexity. Build feedback loops: prototype early, measure actual performance, run load tests, shadow-deploy. Your first-principles model is a hypothesis — treat it like one.

Software Example

You reasoned that in-memory caching eliminates the DB bottleneck. Test it. Maybe the bottleneck was actually network latency, not DB throughput. Reality overrules elegant theory every time.

AI Application

Use AI to steelman your design: "Here's my proposed architecture. What are the 5 ways this could fail in production that I haven't considered?" Then validate those failure modes with real data.

03 / Practical Method

The First Principles
AI Workflow

A step-by-step process for using AI as a Socratic thinking partner — not a code vending machine.

STEP 01 🎯
State the Real Problem

Before touching AI, write one paragraph describing the problem in terms of human or business outcomes — not technical systems. No solutions allowed yet.

"I have a problem where [describe user/business pain].
The current situation is [what's happening now].
The constraint I'm working within is [time/team/budget/tech].
Help me decompose this to its fundamental requirements
without suggesting any solutions yet."
STEP 02 ⚗️
Expose Hidden Assumptions

Ask AI to surface what you're taking for granted. This is the highest-leverage step and most developers skip it entirely.

"Here are the requirements I've gathered:
[paste your requirements]

What assumptions am I making about:
- Scale and load
- Team capabilities
- Technology constraints
- User behavior
Which of these assumptions, if wrong, would most
change the right solution?"
STEP 03 🔭
Generate Solution Space

Only now do you explore implementations — and you explore multiple paths, not the first plausible one.

"Given these verified fundamentals:
[list your non-negotiable constraints]

Generate 3 different approaches to solving this.
For each approach, tell me:
- What assumptions it optimizes for
- Where it breaks down
- What I'd need to be true for it to be the right choice"
STEP 04 ⚔️
Steelman & Challenge

Once you've chosen an approach, make AI argue against it. Force it to find your blind spots before you build.

"I've decided on [your chosen approach].
Act as a skeptical senior engineer.
Give me your strongest argument against this design.
What would break it in production?
What are the hidden costs I'm not seeing?"
STEP 05 🧪
Build, Measure, Revise

Implement with explicit instrumentation planned from day one. Define what "working" means before you write a line of code.

"Help me define success metrics for [feature].
What should I measure to know if my first-principles
reasoning was correct?
What leading indicators would tell me early
if I need to revise my approach?"

05 / Worked Example

Case Study:
Designing a Notification System

Watch first principles thinking applied to a real engineering problem — with AI as a reasoning partner at each step.

📋 PROBLEM STATEMENT
"We need to build a notification system for our app."

Most engineers jump straight to: "Let's use Firebase push notifications" or "Set up a message queue with worker processes." First principles says: stop. Decompose first.

Step 1 → Decompose to fundamentals
→
FUNDAMENTAL TRUTH 1 A notification is: information delivered to a user at a time when they can act on it.
→
FUNDAMENTAL TRUTH 2 Delivery has two axes: channel (email, push, SMS, in-app) and timing (real-time, batched, scheduled).
→
FUNDAMENTAL TRUTH 3 Reliability requirement varies by notification type. "Your payment failed" ≠ "Weekly digest."
→
FUNDAMENTAL TRUTH 4 Users have preferences. Delivery that ignores preferences becomes spam.
Step 2 → Expose assumptions with AI
AI Question: "What scale of notifications? 100/day or 10M/day?
What's the latency requirement? What happens if delivery fails?
Do users configure channels? Are notifications transactional?"

← These questions reveal what you actually need to build.
Step 3 → Reconstruct based on your truths
✓
IF: small scale, single channel, low latency tolerance → Simple DB-polling worker. No queue needed. Dead simple to operate and debug.
✓
IF: high reliability required, multi-channel → Outbox pattern + dedicated delivery workers per channel. Retry logic built in from truth #3.
✓
IF: millions of users, complex preferences → Now the message queue, fan-out architecture, and preference service are justified — because you understand WHY they exist.

The "right" answer depends on your fundamentals. First principles thinking means you choose with full awareness — not by pattern matching to what Amazon or Uber built.

07 / Quick Reference

First Principles
Cheat Sheet

🔍
Questions to Ask First
  • What is the user actually trying to accomplish?
  • What assumptions am I making about scale?
  • Why does the current solution exist?
  • What would I build if I started fresh today?
  • What's the simplest thing that could work?
🤖
AI Prompt Patterns
  • "What assumptions does this solution make?"
  • "Help me decompose this to fundamentals"
  • "Give me 3 approaches with trade-offs"
  • "Steelman the argument against my design"
  • "What would break this in production?"
🚩
Red Flags (Stop Here)
  • "Everyone uses X, so we should too"
  • Copying architecture from a different scale
  • Using AI output without understanding it
  • Optimizing before profiling
  • Solving a solution instead of a problem
✅
The 5-Step Process
  • State the real problem (no solutions)
  • Expose hidden assumptions
  • Generate solution space (3 options)
  • Steelman & challenge your choice
  • Build with measurement from day one
